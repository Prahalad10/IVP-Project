Abstract
Traditional video compression continues to advance, but the gains
in efficiency are diminishing and come at the cost of higher compu-
tational complexity. Despite achieving competitive rate-distortion
results, current neural video codecs (NVCs) generally lack sup-
port for a wide range of quality levels, often requiring multiple
models to achieve flexible rate control, which increases both train-
ing cost and deployment complexity. To address the limitations of
both traditional codecs and current NVCs, we propose a hybrid
video compression framework that integrates traditional codecs
with hint-guided video frame interpolation (VFI), a learning-based
technique for synthesizing intermediate frames. By using decoded
reference frames and leveraging compressed-domain hints to guide
interpolation, our method improves both motion compensation
and reconstruction quality. This design combines the efficiency
of traditional codecs with the adaptability of neural interpolation,
achieving consistent rate-distortion performance and supporting a
wide range of quality levels on standard benchmarks.

1 Introduction
Video has become the dominant form of online content, driven
by the rapid growth of streaming, real-time conferencing, and im-
mersive applications such as virtual and augmented reality. With
ongoing trends toward higher resolutions, frame rates, and richer
content, the demand for efficient video delivery continues to rise.
Given the large share of video traffic, even small improvements in
compression efficiency can yield substantial benefits for storage
and transmission systems.

Traditional video compression standards such as H.264 [36],
HEVC [34], and VVC [3] have been extensively optimized, achiev-
ing excellent rate–distortion performance through hierarchical pre-
diction, refined motion compensation, and bidirectional referencing.
However, these gains come at the cost of increased computational
complexity. Neural video codecs (NVCs) [8, 11–14, 16, 17, 19, 26,
28, 29, 38, 41] have gained attention as alternatives to traditional
codecs by learning spatial-temporal patterns directly from data and
adapting flexibly to diverse content. However, their rate-distortion
trade-off is generally narrow, and most methods require separate
models to cover a wide range of quality levels. This not only in-
creases training cost but also leads to larger overall model size (e.g.,
parameters and weights), which adds to deployment complexity,
particularly in resource-constrained environments.
Video frame interpolation (VFI) offers another way to reduce bi-
trate by transmitting only keyframes and synthesizing intermediate
frames [37]. While this approach can achieve substantial savings
when the interpolated frames are of high fidelity, existing VFI mod-
els often struggle with occlusions, motion blur, or large temporal
gaps, leading to visible artifacts and degraded quality. To overcome
these limitations, we propose a hint-guided video frame interpola-
tion (HGVFI) framework for practical integration into compression
pipelines. In our design, keyframes are encoded using a traditional
codec, while intermediate frames are reconstructed by a neural
interpolation model guided by low-resolution, compressed repre-
sentations of the target frame. These hints provide structural cues
that help the model resolve motion ambiguities and maintain ro-
bustness in challenging scenarios, including degraded references or
large temporal distances, making the method suitable for real-world
video compression.
In summary, our main contributions are: 1) We propose a hint-
guided video frame interpolation (HGVFI) framework that incorpo-
rates compressed keyframes and low-resolution hints for efficient
integration into multimedia systems; 2) We demonstrate that infor-
mative hint frames can effectively compensate for reduced keyframe
quality, enabling accurate and robust interpolation under occlusions
and complex motion; 3) We apply the proposed HGVFI framework
to video compression and validate its effectiveness across diverse
video content, achieving improved rate-distortion performance
compared to representative traditional codecs.

2 Related Work
2.1 Traditional Video Codecs
Traditional video compression standards have undergone several
generations of standardization and refinement. While each newer
version introduced various configurable settings, the core mecha-
nisms have remained the block-based transform coding and motion
estimation. Intra-coded frames, which can be decoded without any

Using Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) and quantization, and by
introducing predictive coded frames (P-frames) and bi-directional
coded frames (B-frames), video codecs can take advantage of tem-
poral redundancy between frames by coding a motion vector and
possibly a residual to the closest visual match. Each newer ver-
sion offered improved coding efficiency at similar quality levels
compared to its predecessor, but also introduced increased computa-
tional complexity. Emerging formats such as 4K/8K resolution and
High Dynamic Range (HDR) video demand significantly higher data
rates, which traditional codecs like H.264/AVC [36] often struggle
to compress efficiently. Newer standards such as H.265/HEVC [34]
and H.266/VVC [3] have been designed to address these demands,
offering improved compression efficiency at the cost of increased
computational complexity and limited support on legacy devices.
2.2 Learning-based Video Codecs
With advances in general-purpose GPUs and deep learning, learned
video compression has emerged as a promising alternatives to tra-
ditional hybrid coding frameworks. Early works such as DVC [19]
replaced each component of conventional codecs, including motion
estimation, prediction, and residual coding, with neural network-
based modules that are jointly optimized under a rate-distortion
objective. This residual-based architecture has since been widely
adopted. Subsequent studies [1, 5, 7, 14–16, 20, 28] further improved
key components such as optical flow estimation, entropy modeling,
and temporal prediction. To better handle complex motion and
improve compensation in the feature domain, techniques including
multi-reference frames, scale-space flow, deformable convolutions,
and attention mechanisms have been introduced.
In parallel, recent efforts [6, 11–13, 18, 22, 26, 31] have shifted
from pixel-domain residual coding to conditional modeling in the
latent space. This strategy enables more flexible use of temporal
context by learning correlations from previously decoded frames,
without relying on explicit residual subtraction. Such latent-space
modeling has led to significant gains in compression efficiency. This
trend is exemplified by the DCVC family of models [11, 12], which
leverage high-dimensional temporal features to improve entropy
estimation and motion compensation.

Our approach shares similarities with Wu et al.’s work [37] by
using interpolation for video compression. However, we do not
adopt a hierarchical structure, nor do we explicitly utilize motion
or residual information. Instead, we introduce a low-resolution
hint frame that conveys coarse content information to guide the
interpolation process. With the keyframes and low-resolution hint
frames encoded using a traditional codec, our framework not only
retains the codec’s flexibility to support a wide range of quality lev-
els, but also achieves better compression efficiency by synthesizing
higher-quality intermediate frames without introducing additional
bitrate overhead.

cluded or fast-moving regions (Figure 1). In contrast, H.264 yields
more coherent results through bidirectional motion compensation
and residuals. While traditional codecs are less expressive than neu-
ral networks, their block-based mechanisms often produce more
stable results under challenging conditions, albeit at lower coding
efficiency.
Building on the above observation, motion and residual infor-
mation are essential for reconstructing high-fidelity frames under
occlusion and large motion. However, integrating such signals into
learned predictive coding frameworks often requires complex archi-
tectures or multiple models to cover diverse quality levels. As an al-
ternative, we introduce hint frames: downsampled and compressed
versions of the target frames encoded with traditional codecs. These
auxiliary frames provide structural guidance for interpolation, en-
abling the model to resolve motion ambiguities and occlusions. Our
hybrid framework combines conventionally encoded keyframes and
hint frames to reconstruct intermediate frames, achieving robust
synthesis with minimal bitrate overhead.

4 Experiment Setup
In this section, we evaluate our proposed approach on both video
frame interpolation and video compression. For frame interpola-
tion, we compare against state-of-the-art methods on datasets in-
cluding Vimeo90K [40] and SNU-FILM [4]. For video compression,
we benchmark our method against widely used industry-standard
codecs, including H.264 [36] and HEVC [34].
To generate training data for our hint-guided interpolation model,
we use H.264 with a constant rate factor (CRF) set to 23. Although
HEVC generally achieves higher compression efficiency, its signifi-
cantly higher computational cost poses challenges for large scale
data processing. Therefore, we use H.264 to compress our training
dataset. During evaluation, we follow the settings used in [11] for
benchmarking H.264 and HEVC (via x264 and x265, respectively)
with FFmpeg in veryslow mode, except that we set the GOP size to
16 to match our framework’s configuration.

4.1 Dataset
4.1.1 Frame Interpolation Dataset. We train our hint-guided VFI
model on the Vimeo90K dataset [40], which is also used by the
baseline EMA-VFI [42]. The training set consists of 51,312 triplets
of 448×256 resolution, each containing three PNG images (im1.png,
im2.png, im3.png), with im2.png typically used as the ground truth.
For evaluation, we use the Vimeo90K test set and the SNU-FILM
dataset, containinh 3,782 triplets in the same format as the training
data [4]. SNU-FILM offers 1,240 triplets at 1280×720 resolution
and defines four difficulty levels (Easy, Medium, Hard, Extreme)
according to the temporal distance between input frames. These
differences enable evaluation under varying motion complexities.
4.1.2 Video Compression Dataset. In order to evaluate the perfor-
mance of our video frame interpolation model in video compression,
we used six videos that have a resolution option of 1920x1080 pixels
from the open Ultra Video Group (UVG) dataset [23]. Each video
has 600 frames and is captured at 120 fps. We used all the frames
from each video for testing. Our final evaluation dataset covers a
diverse set of motion types, including local, global, and crowded
motion. Figure 4(a) shows the thumbnails of the videos selected
for our experiments. Additionally, Figure 4(b) presents the spatial
and temporal complexity of each video, as measured by the Video
Complexity Analyzer [21]. Higher values indicate greater complex-
ity (in either spatial dimension, temporal dimension, or both). As
shown, the selected videos span a wide range of motion and texture
complexity, covering both simple and highly challenging scenarios.
We encoded the videos using a wide range of CRF values, in-
cluding 3, 6, 9, 12, 15, 18, 21, 231, 24, 28, 32, 34, 36, 40, 44, and 48,
with a GOP size of 16. Each GOP was processed individually. In
our setting, each GOP contains only one I-frame at the beginning,
followed by a series of B-frames.
4.2 Experiment Results
4.2.1 Single Frame Interpolation Results. To evaluate our model’s
performance in single-frame interpolation, we compared it against
several state-of-the-art VFI models, including ABME [25], IFR-
Net [9], and EMA-VFI [42], as well as classical methods such as
CAIN [4] and SepConv [24]. As shown in Table 1, our model con-
sistently achieves competitive or superior PSNR and SSIM scores
across multiple datasets.
One major bottleneck in applying VFI directly to video com-
pression is its limited ability to handle complex motion. To assess
our method’s robustness, we evaluate it on the SNU-FILM dataset,
which includes subsets with increasing temporal distances and mo-
tion complexity. Our model performs strongly on the Hard and
Extreme subsets (temporal gaps of 8 and 16 frames), where hint
frames help resolve motion ambiguity and occlusion. On the Easy
subset (temporal gaps of 2 frames), the improvement is smaller, but
our model still achieves a 0.21 dB gain over the baseline.
We further present visual comparisons in Figure 5. In the figure,
the red boxes highlight regions where our model demonstrates
clear advantages. Under challenging conditions such as occlusions
or large motion, methods like EMA-VFI [42] and IFRNet [9] often
produce blurry or distorted frames. H.264, while preserving struc-
tural details, introduces noticeable blocking artifacts. Notably, our
method generates visually sharper and more coherent results.
Unlike other models that operate on pristine input frames, our
method is trained and evaluated on decompressed H.264 sequences
(CRF = 23), which inherently contain compression artifacts and
noise. This setup better reflects real-world scenarios, where trans-
mitted frames are often subject to lossy compression. Despite being
compressed, the hint frames still provide useful motion and ap-
pearance cues, enabling more accurate interpolation. These results
demonstrate that our method is robust to lossy inputs and suitable
for integration into practical video compression pipelines.

5 Conclusion
In this paper, we propose a hint-guided video frame interpolation
(HGVFI) framework tailored for integration into practical video
compression systems. By transmitting full-resolution keyframes
via traditional codecs and reconstructing intermediate frames using
a VFI model guided by carefully selected hint frames, our method
achieves a strong balance between compression efficiency and re-
construction quality. Although the hints are lightweight and com-
pressed, they provide crucial structural cues that enhance interpola-
tion robustness, particularly under challenging scenarios such as oc-
clusions and large temporal distances. Extensive experiments show
that our framework surpasses traditional codecs in rate-distortion
performance, confirming its effectiveness in real-world applications.
Furthermore, by leveraging conventional codecs, our approach nat-
urally supports a wide range of quality levels without requiring
multiple models, addressing a key limitation of current neural video
codecs. Moving forward, we plan to explore more compact and
adaptive hint generation strategies to further reduce bitrate while
preserving visual fidelity, with the goal of making the framework
more efficient and broadly deployable.
